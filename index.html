Preamble

(i)Dean Park Primary School Parent Council is the Parent Council established for Dean Park Primary school in Balerno, Edinburgh.

(ii)The Parent Council is established by the school's Parent Forum under section 6 of the Scottish Schools (Parental Involvement) Act 2006.

(iii)The school's Parent Forum adopted this constitution as the constitution of the Parent Council on [date of adoption]. It supersedes the constitution adopted on 24 May 2016.

(iv)Anyone interested in the functions and duties of the Parent Council should also refer to the Scottish Schools (Parental Involvement) Act 2006, in particular:

  • section 8, which sets out the functions of the Parent Council,
  • section 9, which gives the school's headteacher both the right and the duty to attend Parent Council meetings and requires that those meetings generally be held in public,
  • section 10, which sets out the Parent Council's financial powers,
  • section 13, which sets out the duty of the headteacher of the school to report at least once a year to the Parent Council,
  • section 14, which provides for the Parent Council to have a role in appointing the school's headteacher and deputy headteachers.

Part 1
Operations of the Parent Council

Decision making by the Council

b001Decisions at meetings

(1)Decisions of the Parent Council are to be taken at a meeting of the Council as follows.

(2)The member of the Council presiding over the meeting is to propose a decision and invite the other members present to indicate whether they agree with it.

(3)If any of those members express disagreement, the presiding member must invite them to vote on the proposed decision.

(4)Otherwise, the proposed decision is adopted as the decision of the Council.

(5)In the event of a vote on a proposed decision:

(a)the proposed decision is adopted as the Council's decision if a simple majority of the members present agree with it,

(b)if the votes are tied, and the decision in question is not an appointment decision, the presiding member has a casting vote,

(c)if the votes are tied, and the decision in question is an appointment decision, whether or not the proposed decision is adopted as the Council's decision is to be decided in a manner:

(i)that is chosen by the presiding member, and

(ii)which, in the presiding member's opinion, renders the outcome unforeseeable (for example, a coin toss).

(6)In paragraph (5), "an appointment decision" means a decision to:

(a)co-opt a person as a member of the Council, or

(b)appoint a person as an office holder of the Council.

(7)For the avoidance of doubt, the presiding member may:

(a)express disagreement for the purpose of paragraph (3),

(b)vote differently for the purposes of sub-paragraphs (a) and (b) of paragraph 5.

Current constitution suggests a vote's needed on every decision. This may be wordier than what the current constitution says, but hopefully it's simpler in practice because it means there doesn't need to be a vote if nobody's saying no.

b002Decisions outwith meetings

The code of conduct alludes to another mode of decision making whereby decisions are taken by office bearers and reported back. Is provision needed for that?

Is there also a need to allow the Council to take decisions with neither a formal delegation of authority nor a meeting? (e.g. if the members all agree something on Whatsapp)

If so, should there be any restrictions on the sorts of decisions that can be taken outwith the context of a meeting - e.g. spending decisions?

b011aMember with conflict of interest excluded from decision

(1)If a member of the Council has a conflict of interest in relation to a matter about which the Council is making a decision, that member is to play no part in making the decision.

(2)For the purposes of this article, a person has a conflict of interest in relation to a matter if the person has a connection to it (direct or indirect) which would raise in the mind of a fair-minded observer a suspicion that, in dealing with the matter, the person may put other interests ahead of those of the Council.

(3)Being the parent of a child who is attending the school, has attended it, or can be expected to attend it, cannot create a conflict of interest for the purposes of this article.

The current conflict of interest rule only deals with conflicts at meetings, which doesn't allow for the possibility of decisions being taken outwith meetings.

I've suggested a broad description of what a conflict of interest is, partly to avoid reproducing what the current constitution says about who's connected with a council member. It's a very odd proposition because it doesn't bear to be exhaustive and begs more questions than it answers, at least to my mind.

Current constitution says being staff member can't create a conflict of interest. Necessary at the moment because staff are ex officio members. Desirable going forward? If a Council member also happens to be a teacher and the Council's discussing something relevant to teaching conditions, should that be a declarable conflict?

Council meetings

b003Ordinary meetings

(1)It is for the Parent Council to decide when to meet.

(2)At least 2 meetings should take place in each school term.

b004Special meetings

(1)The chair of the Council may arrange a meeting of the Council at any time.

(2)If at least 3 members of the Parent Council make a request to the chair to have a meeting of the Council on a particular subject, the chair must:

(a)arrange a meeting to take place within 14 days of receiving the request, or

(b)decline the request.

(3)The chair may only decline a request to have a meeting on a particular subject if:

(a)the chair considers the subject to be one that it would be grossly inappropriate for the Council to discuss,

(b)the subject is one that has been discussed at a Council meeting in the [6 months] prior to the request and the chair is satisfied that there has been no material change in circumstances since the Council last discussed it, or

(c)the subject is the decision of the chair to decline a previous request to have a meeting under this article.

(4)If the chair declines a request to have a meeting on a particular subject, the chair must, without delay, inform all members of the Council of:

(a)the request, and

(b)the reasons for the chair's decision to decline it.

The current constitution has no mechanism to rebuff a request by 3 council members for what it calls an extraordinary meeting? Is the requirement to get 3 members to back the call safeguard enough against abuse? In case not, I have offered a limited power for the chair to say no to a special meeting. The grounds on which the chair can say no are constrained and the chair has to tell all members of the decision to say no. If members are unhappy with the chair's reasoning, they can get that on the agenda for the next ordinary meeting (see article b015), but what they can't do is request another extraordinary meeting to discuss the refusal of the last request because then there'd be a risk of getting stuck in a never ending loop.

If this restriction on requests for special meetings is wanted, is a 6 month period of limitations on retreading the same ground ok?

b015Meeting agenda

(1)It is for the chair to prepare the agenda for meetings of the Council.

(2)If the meeting is being arranged as a result of a request to have a meeting on a particular subject under article b004, that subject must be on the meeting's agenda.

(3)If a member of the Parent Forum requests that a particular subject be on the agenda of a Council meeting, that subject must be included on the agenda for a meeting unless:

(a)the chair considers the subject to be one that it would be grossly inappropriate for the Council to discuss, or

(b)the subject is one that has been discussed at a Council meeting in the [6 months] prior to the request and the chair is satisfied that there has been no material change in circumstances since the Council last discussed it.

(4)If paragraph (3) requires that a particular subject be included on the agenda for a Council meeting:

(a)it is to be included on the agenda for the first meeting to take place at least [10 days] after the request is made, but

(b)it may be included on the agenda of an earlier meeting if the chair considers that appropriate.

This is a stab at giving the chair some way of keeping meetings somewhat on the rails by at least stopping probelmatic, or repetitious, subjects getting on the agenda. It doesn't stop things being raised as AOB at the end of a meeting, but coming at the end of the meeting gives the presiding member the way out of ending the meeting rather than trying to eject someone.

The 10 days proposed in paragraph (4)(b) is to accommodate the fact that the agenda has to be ready at the point of giving notice of the meeting - see article b005 for the timescales for that.

I haven't expressly included a requirement to allow for AOB at the end of the agenda of at least all ordinary meetings. Would it be desirable to spell that out? The current constitution says that any member of the Parent Forum can raise any topic for discussion at a meeting and that any other business may be transacted at an ordinary meeting. Perhaps some will feel that the provisions allowing Forum members to have things put on the agenda is a watering down of that entitlement if it's not made explicit that they'll also get a chance at the end of meetings.

The current constitution's rule that AOB will be taken says it's at the chair's discretion, but if there isn't time for it it has to go on the agenda for the next meeting. I've left out that requirement to put it on the agenda for the next meeting. The person who wants to discuss at the next meeting can request that under paragraph (3), which comes with a safety valve for problem agenda items.

b005Public notice of meeting

(1)Public notice of a Parent Council meeting must be given:

(a)at least 7 days before the meeting in the case of an ordinary meeting (that is a meeting that has not been arranged in accordance with article b004),

(b)at least 4 days before the meeting in the case of a special meeting (that is a meeting that has been arranged in accordance with article b004).

(2)Notice of a meeting must state:

(a)the date and time of the meeting,

(b)the location of the meeting (unless it is to be held virtually),

(c)details for joining the meeting remotely (if applicable), and

(d)the agenda for the meeting.

(3)It is for the Council to decide how to give public notice of a meeting.

(4)In considering how to give public notice of a meeting, the Council must have particular regard to the importance of ensuring that members of the Parent Forum are aware of it.

Currently the constitution only deals with giving notice to Council members. That seems odd given that it also says that any member of the Parent Forum can raise an issue at a meeting and that meetings are to be public. So I've suggested a requirement to give notice to the world, rather than just members. How that's fulfilled is left to the Council. A post on the website, Facebook or what have you would surely do. Are you content with this?

b006Invitation to meeting

(1)The Council must invite the following people to each of its meetings:

(a)the headteacher of the school

(b)the headteacher of Balerno High School,

(c)the councillors elected for the wards that fall within the school's catchment area.

(2)The Council may invite anyone else it wishes.

(3)In considering whom to invite, the Council should in particular consider inviting:

(a)the head boy and girl of the school,

(b)members of the [pupil council],

(c)members of any community council operating within the school's catchment area,

(d)an officer of the education authoirty,

(e)the members of the Scottish Parliament returned for the constituency, and the region, within which the school sits,

(f)the member of Parliament (Westminster) returned for the constituency within which the school sits.

(4)In this article, "catchment area" has the meaning given in section 28A(3D) of the Education (Scotland) Act 1980.

The list of mandatory invitees replaces the current constition's class of non-voting members. The headteacher's representative isn't mentioned, because the invite can be sent to the headteacher and then it's up to her whether to send a representative - the meetings are open to all anyway.

The current constitution's class of non-voting members also includes a representative of every sub-group. As we discussed I don't really know what it means by sub-group. Is there anyone, like the head of the PTA, who should be on the mandatory invite list who was previously caught by that limb of the non-voting members category?

Pupil council is in square brackets because I don't know what it is and need to consider further whether a definition should be added.

The list of people to consider inviting (paragraph (3)) is much the same as in the current constitution. A couple of points to note:

  • The current constitution mentions a rep of the Scottish Government. I haven't included that. Should I?
  • The current constitution refers to the local MSP. Of course we have a constitutency and regional MSPs, I've assumed consideration should be given to inviting them all.
  • The current constitution says that local councillors should be considered as possible invitees. But it also makes them non-voting members, which means they have to be given notice of the meetings anyway. I've omitted them from the list on the basis they're already mandatory invitees.
  • Are we ok with the concept of head boy and head girl? Nobody agitating about the position of non-binary pupils?

b007Quorum

(1)A meeting of the Council is quorate (that is, it may proceed) if, at a minimum, the following are present:

(a)one third of the Council's members,

(b)the chair or vice chair of the Council, and

(c)a member who is not the chair or vice chair.

(2)If the offices of chair and vice chair are both vacant, the requirement for one of those office holders to be present does not apply.

(3)When calculating one third of the Council's members for the purposes of this article, any remainder is to be disregarded.

b008Presiding

(1)If the chair of the Council is present at a meeting of the Council, the chair is to preside over the meeting.

(2)If:

(a)the chair of the Council is not present, and

(b)the vice chair of the Council is present,

the vice chair is to preside over the meeting.

(3)If the offices of chair and vice chair are both vacant, the oldest member present is to preside over the meeting.

The current constitution doesn't have a way of dealing with the situtation of there being no chair or vice chair. It's probably unlikely it'll happen, but it would be a real problem if it did because the current constitution says the Council has to appoint members to those posts but the Council can't meet to do so if there's no chair or vice chair because of the quoracy rule (you'll notice I've added some latitude there too). Absent a chair or vice chair I've suggested the oldest member presides meantime - it's not totally comfortable going by age but what else can be used? Age has been used in situations like this for some serious public bodies, recently it was how we catered for the possibility of the chair of the Parole Board going down with Covid.

b009Record keeping

(1)Every meeting of the Parent Council is to be minuted.

(2)If the secretary of the Council is present at the meeting, the secretary is to take the minutes.

(3)If the secretary is not present (or the office of secretary is vacant), the presding member must appoint someone to take the minutes.

When we met a distaste for the current constitution's term 'clerk' was expressed. Is secretary preferable? Clerk is the term the 2006 Act uses, and there are some legal consequences to being 'the clerk', but if secretary is preferred we can just explain in the interpretation provision at the end that secretary is clerk in 2006 Act terms.

b010Attendance at meetings

(1)Council meetings are generally to be open to the public.

(2)But during a meeting the Council may decide to deal with a matter confidentially.

(3)While that matter is being dealt with, the only people entitled to attend are:

(a)the members of the Council, and

(b)either:

(i)the headteacher of the school, or

(ii)if the headteacher has elected to be represented at the meeting by someone else, the headteacher's representative.

It's worth noting, this is really just a repeat of the law - see section 9 of the Parental Involvement Act.

We discussed giving the presiding member a power to remove members from meetings, but section 9 doesn't allow that and the constitution can't supersede the law in this area. Using the chair's agenda setting power, as discussed above, is the best I can come up with for keeping meetings in check.

b011Member with conflict of interest at meeting

(1)A member attending a Council meeting who is aware of having a conflict of interest in relation to a matter being discussed at the meeting must:

(a)inform those present at the meeting of the conflict, and

(b)take no part in the discussion of the matter.

(2)Where a member has informed those present at a meeting that the member has a conflict of interest in relation to a matter, for so long as the matter is under discussion, the meeting is to be conducted as though the member were not present for the purposes of:

(a)article b001, and

(b)article b008.

(3)For the purposes of this article, references to having a conflict of interest in relation to a matter are to be construed in the same way as in article b011a.

Current constitution says member with conflict of interest has to withdraw from meeting. Not clear what that means. If it means leave, I don't think that's legal standing what the 2006 Act says about the rights of people to attend council meetings.

Should the person be barred from taking part in discussion of the matter? Barring from voting is one thing, but saying they can't talk about the issue seems extreme. Oddly it means a member's in a worst position than a member of the parent Forum who can presumably turn up and say whatever regardless of conflict of interest. If the member's stated they have a conflict, perhaps that's enough because others will know he or she is speaking against that background?

Note the fact someone isn't participating doesn't mean they're to be ignored for the purpose of deciding if the meeting's quorate. Is that ok?

Not participating does affect the rule about who's to preside, so if the chair had a conflict would have to stop presiding over that part of the meeting. Is that ok? The current constitution doesn't spell it out, but I assume that's part of what withdrawing from a meeting means.

Part 2
Members and office holders

a001Number of members

(1)The Council may not have more than 14 members.

(2)If there are fewer than 4 members, the Council must make all reasonable efforts to have at least 4 members as soon as practicable.

a002Appointment

A person becomes a member of the Council by being either:

(a)appointed through the routine appointment process (see article a003), or

(b)co-opted in accordance with article a004.

a003Routine appointment process

(1)If on [1 June] in any year it is foreseeable that the Council will have fewer than the maximum permitted number of members on the first day of term after the summer holidays that year, the Council must invite members of the Parent Forum to apply to become members of the Council.

(2)It is for the Council to decide:

(a)how to invite applications, and

(b)the deadline for applications.

(3)If, by the deadline for applications, the number of eligible applicants is less than or equal to the anticipated number of vacancies, those applicants become members of the Council on the first day of term after the summer holidays that year.

(4)If, by the deadline for applications, the number of eligible applicants is greater than the anticipated number of vacancies, an election is to be held to determine which of those applicants will become members of the Council.

(5)Schedule 1 sets out the election process.

(6)For the purposes of this article:

(a)a person is an eligible applicant if the person:

(i)has a reasonable expectation of being a member of the Parent Forum on the first day of term after the summer holidays, and

(ii)has not been removed from the Council under article a006 within [18 months] of the deadline for applications,

(b)"anticipated number of vacancies" means the difference between:

(i)the maximum permitted number of Council members, and

(ii)the number of members the Council expects it will have on the first day of term after the summer holidays unless members are appointed through the routine appointment process.

a004Co-option of members

(1)The Council may decide to co-opt a person as a member if there are fewer than the maximum permitted number of members.

(2)The Council may only co-opt as a member a person who is a member of the Parent Forum.

a005Tenure

(1)A person appointed as a member of the Council remains a member until any of the following occurs:

(a)the person's period of appointment expires,

(b)the person informs the Council that the person resigns,

(c)the person ceases to be a member of the Parent Forum,

(d)the Parent Forum decide to remove the person from the Council (see article a006).

(2)The period of appointment of a person appointed as a member through the routine appointment process expires immediately before the first day of the new school year in the year falling 4 years after the year in which the person made the application that led to the appointment.

(3)The period of appointment of a person co-opted as a member expires immediately before the first day of term after the summer holidays following the person's co-option.

This is a bit different from current constitution. The stuff about appointing up to 7 members every 2 years and inviting applications for appointment every 2 years is gone. What's the point of it? If there are going to be vacancies, why not just fill however many there are?

I've suggested getting rid of the idea that a co-opted member serves out the end of the term of the routinely appointed member that the co-opted member replaces. In the first place, it's not clear that every co-opted member will be replacing a routinely appointed member. Also, if multiple routinely appointed members stand down at once, who decides which co-opted member is a replacement for whom? And things get really messy if co-opted members get brought on to replace co-opted members. So why not say co-opted members come on board till the start of the new school year? That'll mean there's an imminent vacancy, which will trigger the requirement to invite routine application appointments in June. The co-opted member can then apply and become a normal member like everyone else from the start of the new school year - or not in the unlikely event that there's a vote and forum members choose someone else &emdash; it seems only democratic to allow for the possibility.

Should there be an ability to take a resignation from someone without a power of attorney for the resigning member? Unlikely to come up often, but would seem unwelcome to have to go to forum in those circumstances.

a006Removal of member

(1)A person may be removed as a member of the Council only by a decision of the Parent Forum at an extraordinary meeting of the Forum called by the Council to consider the person's removal from membership.

(2)The Council may call an extraordinary meeting to consider a person's removal from membership only if the Council has:

(a)decided that the person is unable or unfit to fulfil the person's role as a member, and

(b)given the person an opportunity to resign in light of that decision.

(3)Reasons for the Council to decide that a person is unfit to fulfil the person's role as a member include:

(a)the person's failure to abide by the code of conduct set out in schedule 2,

(b)the person's failure, without good reason, to attend:

(i)any Council meetings over a period of 6 months or more, or

(ii)3 or more consecutive Council meetings.

On reflection, code of conduct shouldn't be a schedule to constitution but a separate document which the constitution refers to. The process for changing the constitution is perhaps too burdensome for changing the code. I had thought to put in a simplified route to changing the constitution's schedules, but looking again at the 2006 Act I realise that wouldn't be lawful.

a007Offices

(1)The Council is to have the following offices:

(a)a chair,

(b)a vice chair, and

(c)a secretary.

(2)The Council may decide to have other offices too.

(3)Secretary is the only office of the Council that may be held by a person who is not a member.

(4)It is for the Council to decide whom to appoint to an office.

(5)An appointment to an office ends:

(a)2 years after it began,

(b)when the office holder resigns, or

(c)(except in the case of the secretary) when the office holder ceases to be a member of the Council.

(6)A person may be appointed more than once, and consecutively, to the same office.

a008Performance of the chair's functions by someone else

(1)The chair may delegate to another member of the Council any function that this constitution confers on the chair.

(2)If:

(a)the chair is unavailable to discharge a function conferred on the chair by this constitution (for example due to ill health), and

(b)the chair has not delegated the function to another member,

the function is to be discharged by the vice chair or, if the vice chair is unavailable to charge the function, by the oldest member of the Council.

a009Interpretation of Part

References to the first day of term after the summer holidays in any year is to that year's school commencement date for the school fixed under section 32 of the Education (Scotland) Act 1980.

Part 3
The Parent Forum

c002How the Forum makes decisions

(1)A decision is taken by the Parent Forum, for the purposes of article c001 if a motion proposing the decision wins the required level of support at either:

(a)an annual general meeting of the Forum, or

(b)an extraordinary meeting of the Forum.

(2)The required level of support is the votes of at least two thirds of the sum of:

(a)the number of members of the Forum attending the meeting, and

(b)the number of proxy votes cast in relation to the motion.

c003Requirement for notice of a motion before vote

(1)A motion may be voted on at a meeting of the Parent Forum only if the Council has made reasonable efforts to give notice of the motion to the members of the Forum at least:

(a)21 days before the motion if the motion calls on the Forum to decide to modify or replace this constitution, or

(b)14 days before the meeting in any other case.

(2)Notice of a motion must include:

(a)the terms of the motion, and

(b)an explanation of how a perosn can have someone else (a proxy) vote in respect of the motion at the meeting on the person's behalf.

I've proposed standardising the notice period for non-constitutional motions. Under the current constitution it's 7 days' notice for a motion at AGM but 14 days at EGM. Why the difference?

c004Annual General Meeting

(1)The Council must arrange an annual general meeting of the Parent Forum in September or October each year.

(2)The Council must make reasonable efforts to give the members of the Forum at least 28 days' notice of the meeting.

(3)The agenda for the meeting must include the following:

(a)a report from the chair of the Council on:

(i)the Council's activities since the last annual general meeting, and

(ii)its plans for the next 12 months,

(b)a discussion and vote on any motion that a member of the Forum has requested the Council to put on the agenda provided that:

(i)the request is made at least [21 days] before the meeting,

(ii)the Council is satisfied that the request has the support of at least 25 members of the Forum (counting the proposer), and

(iii)the proposed motion does not call on the Forum to decide to remove a person as a member of the Council (see article a006).

Current constitution refers to a chair's report. I've set out what I think that means. Is it right?

Should it be possible for forum members to propose things to discuss without needing to have an associated motion?

Should there be an ability for forum members proposals for discussion topics and/or motions to be rejected if, say, they're potentially defamatory?

I've omitted the current constitution's requirement to have an annual review of the constitution at the AGM. Is that something that happens? Is it useful?

c005Extraordinary Meeting

(1)The Parent Forum must arrange an extraordinarymeeting of the Parent Forum if at least 25 members of the Forum request the Council to arrange such a meeting to discuss and vote on a motion.

(2)Despite paragraph (1), the Council need not arrange a meeting if the proposed motion would call on the Forum to decide to remove a person as a member of the Council (see article a006).

(3)The Council must make reasonable efforts to:

(a)hold the meeting as soon as practicable, and

(b)give members of the Forum notice of the meeting.

c006Notice of a meeting of the Forum

Notice of a meeting of the Parent Forum must state:

(a)the date and time of the meeting,

(b)the location of the meeting (unless it is to be held virtually), and

(c)details for joining the meeting remotely (if applicable).

c007How requests are to be made to the Council

A request to the Council for the purposes of this Part is to be made by ...

This Part talks about forum members requesting the Council to arrange EGMs and put things on meeting agendas. It might be worth saying here how those requests are to be made, e.g. are they to be emailed? Can they be made verbally by chatting to a Council member?
